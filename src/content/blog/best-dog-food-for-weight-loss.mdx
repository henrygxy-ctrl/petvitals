## The Scope of the Problem

According to the Association for Pet Obesity Prevention's latest survey, **56% of dogs in the U.S. are classified as overweight or obese** — approximately 50 million dogs. This isn't just an aesthetic issue. Excess weight shortens lifespan by an average of **2.5 years** and contributes to:

- Osteoarthritis and joint degeneration
- Insulin resistance and diabetes
- Cardiovascular strain
- Respiratory compromise (especially in brachycephalic breeds)
- Increased surgical and anesthetic risk
- Reduced quality of life — overweight dogs are less active, play less, and tire faster

The good news: even modest weight loss (6–8% of body weight) produces measurable improvements in mobility and metabolic markers. It's one of the most impactful things you can do for your dog's health.

## First: Determine If Your Dog Is Overweight

Use the Body Condition Score (BCS) system. On a 9-point scale:
- **1–3**: Underweight — ribs, spine, and pelvic bones prominent
- **4–5**: Ideal — ribs palpable with slight fat cover, visible waist from above, abdominal tuck from the side
- **6–7**: Overweight — ribs palpable with difficulty, waist barely visible
- **8–9**: Obese — ribs not palpable, no waist, fat deposits over spine and tail base

If you can't easily feel your dog's ribs with light pressure, they're overweight.

## Calculating Calorie Targets for Weight Loss

Use our [feeding calculator](/feeding-calculator) for a precise number, but here's the quick formula:

1. Calculate Resting Energy Requirement (RER): `RER = 70 × (body weight in kg)^0.75`
2. For weight loss: feed **RER × 1.0** (this is 70–80% of maintenance), or target 1–2% body weight loss per week
3. Example: a 25 kg dog needs RER = 70 × 25^0.75 ≈ 790 kcal/day for weight loss

All treats, dental chews, and table scraps count toward this total. They add up fast — one tablespoon of peanut butter is ~95 calories, equivalent to 12% of a small dog's daily allowance.

## What to Look For in Weight-Loss Dog Food

**High protein (>30% on dry matter basis)**: Preserves lean muscle mass during caloric deficit. Muscle burns more calories at rest than fat does.

**Moderate to low fat under 12% dry matter**: Fat contains 2.25× the calories per gram of protein or carbohydrate. Reducing fat density allows larger, more satisfying portions.

**Added L-carnitine**: This amino acid helps transport fatty acids into mitochondria for energy production. Clinical studies show it supports fat loss while preserving muscle.

**Fiber**: Adds bulk without calories, increasing satiety. Look for beet pulp, cellulose, or psyllium.

**Key terms on labels**: "Weight Management," "Healthy Weight," "Light," or "Less Active." But don't trust the label alone — read the guaranteed analysis and compare kcal/cup. The calorie density of "weight management" foods varies wildly between brands.

## The Feeding Strategy

1. **Measure everything** with a kitchen scale — cups are inaccurate. A "cup" can vary by 20% depending on how densely you scoop
2. **Feed 2–3 meals per day** rather than free-feeding
3. **Use a portion of the meal as training treats** — preserves the bond while controlling calories
4. **Low-calorie treat alternatives**: baby carrots, green beans (no salt), cucumber slices, air-popped popcorn (plain)
5. **Weigh your dog weekly** at the same time of day. 1–2% loss per week is the target

## Exercise: The Multiplier

Diet drives weight loss; exercise accelerates it and preserves muscle. Start with what your dog can do comfortably — an overweight dog shouldn't be thrown into a 5K. Low-impact options:
- Swimming (zero joint stress, high calorie burn)
- Multiple short walks rather than one long one
- Food puzzles that make them work for meals
- Nose work games — 15 minutes of scent work is as tiring as an hour of walking

## When to See the Vet

Unexplained weight gain despite calorie restriction warrants investigation. Hypothyroidism and Cushing's disease both cause weight gain and are common in middle-aged and senior dogs. Conversely, rapid weight loss (>2% per week) or loss of muscle mass suggests the deficit is too aggressive.

## A Note on Patience

A 20 kg dog needs to lose 4 kg to reach ideal weight. At 1% per week, that's ~20 weeks — nearly 5 months. This is slow work. Your dog didn't gain the weight in a month and won't lose it in one either. Consistency beats intensity every time.
